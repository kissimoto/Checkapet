Glossary

What Tests Should I Advise?

Each breed has their own set of available tests and not all tests need to be run.

An informed decision using guidance such as Estimated Breed Value (EBV) schemes, BVA schemes and common diseases in the breed are recommended.

Consideration should be given to the size of the gene pool for the breed, so as not to unnecessarily exclude individuals that can benefit a small gene pool.

Some tests require a blood sample, others a mouth swab. BVA schemes may require radiographs to be submitted and brachycephalics benefit from BOAS functional grading using the exercise tolerance test or whole body barometrics.

Why test?

“Unfortunately the restrictive breeding patterns that have developed as part and parcel of the purebred dog scene have not be without collateral damage to all breeds. Increasingly, inherited diseases are imposing a serious disease burden on most, if not all, breeds of dogs” Dr Jeff Sampson KC genetic advisor Judicious use of DNA tests may hold the solution, and cross breeding away from purebred dogs to more natural looking individuals.

    Official Health Schemes

    Radiographs taken by a vet and sent for assessment by a panel of specialists, who check for abnormal formation of the elbow joints. Results from 0 (unaffected), 1, 2 or 3 (severely affected).

    Radiographs are taken by a vet (from 12 months old in the UK and 24 months old in the USA) and sent for assessment by a panel of specialists, who check for hip conformation. Each hip is scored individually from 0–52, resulting in a total score out of 104. A certificate will be issued with the score for each hip.

    The scheme is open to all dogs and is not restricted to those registered with the Kennel Club. All breeds should be considered.

    Example: 32% of Pekingese dogs are affected by hip dysplasia (HD).

    The HD score, and its relation to the median score for the breed, should help guide breeding decisions:

    • Breed from dogs and bitches with scores lower than the breed average.
    • Remember that dogs may appear clinically sound but still have high HD scores.

    Hip Dysplasia (HD) and Elbow Dysplasia (ED) radiographs may be submitted together for assessment under the BVA/KC scheme. Submissions can be made through BVA's online submission portal.

    A veterinary ophthalmologist checks for multiple inherited and physical abnormalities that can cause progressive blindness or pain.

    Examples include:

    • Gonioscopy tests for goniodysgenesis;
    • Pectinate ligament abnormality (PLA) assesses a predisposition for primary closed angle glaucoma, a painful progressive eye disease leading to blindness. Gonioscopy should be carried out every three years to assess progression of PLA.
    • Hereditary cataract - opacity of the lens causing disturbed vision

    What is a BAER hearing test for dogs?

    The Brainstem Auditory Evoked Response (BAER) test, also known as Brainstem Evoked Potential (BSEP) testing, is a hearing assessment used to evaluate hearing and brainstem function in dogs and cats. A clicking sound is played through an earphone into the dog's ear canal, and electrodes on the head measure the nerve response. The range of frequencies tested is the same as for human hearing.

    How long does a BAER test take?

    Each ear is tested individually, and the procedure typically takes 15–20 minutes. It is advised to test puppies' hearing at 5 to 6 weeks of age, although BAER testing can be performed at any age.

    Putnam scoring for patella (knee cap) subluxation
    - examination by a vet to determine if the knee caps are unstable. Grading from 0-4.

    The most common breeds diagnosed with patella luxation are the Yorkshire Terrier (n = 116, 13.6 % of all cases), Chihuahua (70, 8.2 %), Jack Russell Terrier (70, 8.2 %) and Cavalier King Charles Spaniel (52, 6.1 %), as well as 174 (20.4 %) crossbreds.

    It is not uncommon in small breeds, to present with a ‘slipping’ or ‘subluxating patella’. These dogs may suddenly carry one hind leg for a short distance, then carry on normally.

    The patella rests in a groove in the thigh bone and normally moves up and down in the groove, but never sideways.

    If it dislocates sideways, the dog is unable to weight-bear normally and over time the knee joint can become arthritic. The cruciate ligament in the knee is then at risk from damage and pain.

    How is the scoring performed?

    You will be asked a couple of questions regarding your dogs movement and any history of lameness, limping, pain etc. The vet will view your dog standing at rest as well as walking. They will then feel the patella and the knee joint.

    The system used to classify the results of the examination are the;

    Putnam Patellar Luxation Grading System for Dogs

    • Grade 0: Normal
    • Grade 1: The patella can be manually dislocated only with the knee in full extension, but when pressure is released without manipulation of the limb the patella regains its original position. Spontaneous luxation of the patella during normal joint motion rarely occurs in these dogs. These dogs present as ‘normal’ / never lame, but might move ‘oddly’ at times.
    • Grade 2: The patella can be completely dislocated, but manipulation of the hind limb causes the patella to regain its original position immediately. On physical examination, the patella dislocates easily, especially when the paw is rotated.
    • Grade 3: The patella is found spontaneously dislocated with the animal in a standing position. Or it is ‘permanently’ dislocated but can be repositioned manually. In these dogs the patella groove is too shallow – this can be palpated at the time. These dogs sometimes carry the affected limb some of the time.
    • Grade 4: The patella is permanently dislocated and cannot be repositioned manually. These dogs are not be able to walk normally, or might move in a crouched position with both limbs partially flexed. Some carry the affected limb all or some of the time. The patella groove is shallow, absent, or even convex.

    Commonly used for Pugs, French Bulldogs and English Bulldogs, but advised in all brachycephalic breeds.

    Inheritance of the Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome (BOAS) is complex and not fully understood. Conformational factors may increase the risk of BOAS including stenotic nares, thick/short necks, short and wide skull, short muzzle in proportion to the head. The scheme gives a score for a dog and the advice is to breed from those with a score of 0 or 1 so as to reduce the chances of their puppies suffering from BOAS and its associated problems. The score may increase with age.

    Both parents should be tested regularly, though it is not a guarantee.

    Grading
    • 0 Green: Clinically unaffected - The lowest risk of breeding dogs affected by BOAS
    • 1 Green: Clinically unaffected but with mild signs - Low risk
    • 2 Amber: Clinically affected with moderate signs of BOAS - A higher risk of breeding dogs affected by BOAS
    • 3 Red: Clinically affected with severe signs of BOAS - Likely to produce puppies with BOAS, these matings are not recommended.

    Estimated Breed Values (EBV) for hips and elbows

    A way to assess how much of a particular condition has been inherited and the likelihood of passing it on to the puppies.

    EBVs are only currently available for hip dysplasia and elbow dysplasia in a limited range of breeds. There is no need for the dog to be KC registered.

    Currently 28 breeds have EBVs for hip dysplasia and 15 breeds have EBVs for elbow dysplasia:

    Airdale Terrier, Akita, Alaskan Malamute, Bearded Collie, Bernese Mountain Dog, Border Collie, Clumber Spaniel, Dogue de Bordeaux, English Setter, Flat Coated Retriever, German Shepherd Dog, Golden Retriever, Gordon Setter, Hungarian Puli, Hungarian Vizsla, Irish Setter, Italian Spinone, Labrador Retriever, Large Munsterlander, Leonberger, Newfoundland, Old English Sheepdog, Rhodesian Ridgeback, Rottweiler, Samoyed, Siberian Husky, Tibetan Terrier, Weimaraner and Welsh Springer Spaniel.

    Six breeds have EBVs for both hip score and elbow grade: Newfoundlands, Labrador Retrievers, Golden Retrievers, German Shepherd Dogs, Rottweilers and Bernese Mountain Dogs.

    Where available, EBVs should be used to assist breeding decisions and breeders should adhere to Kennel Club guidance on its Mate Select online facility.
