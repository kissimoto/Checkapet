What Tests Should I Advise?

Each breed has their own set of available tests. Ensure you're recommending the right health screenings for your patients.

Find all available tests

Not all tests need to be run, but an informed decision using guidance such as Estimated Breed Value (EBV) schemes, BVA schemes and common diseases in the breed are recommended.

Consideration should be given to the size of the gene pool for the breed, so as not to unnecessarily exclude individuals that can benefit a small gene pool.

Some tests require a blood sample, others a mouth swab. BVA schemes may require radiographs to be submitted and brachycephalics benefit from BOAS functional grading using the exercise tolerance test or whole body barometrics.

Official Health Schemes

Radiographs taken by a vet and sent for assessment by a panel of specialists, who check for abnormal formation of the elbow joints. Results from 0 (unaffected), 1, 2 or 3 (severely affected).

Radiographs taken (from 12 months old in the UK, 24 months old in USA) by a vet and sent for assessment by a panel of specialists, who check for hip conformation. Each hip is scored from 0-52. Total score out of 104. A certificate will be issued with the score for each hip. The scheme is open to all dogs and is not restricted to those which are registered with the Kennel Club. All breeds should be considered e.g. 32% Pekingese have HD. The HD score and its relation to the median for the breed should help guide which dogs to breed from and which to avoid. Breed from dogs and bitches with scores lower than average for their breed. Remember that dogs may be clinically sound but have high HD scores. HD and ED radiographs may be submitted together for the BVA/KC scheme. https://chs.bva.co.uk/

A veterinary ophthalmologist checks for multiple inherited and physical abnormalities that can cause progressive blindness or pain. To find a panellist; https://www.bva.co.uk/canine-health-schemes/eye-scheme/find-an-eye-panellist/ e.g. Gonioscopy tests for goniodysgenesis; Pectinate ligament abnormality (PLA) assesses a predisposition for primary closed angle glaucoma, a painful progressive eye disease leading to blindness. Gonioscopy should be carried out every three years to assess progression of PLA. Hereditary cataract - opacity of the lens causing disturbed vision Distichiasis - misplaced eyelashes that rub on the eyeball Persistent Hyperplastic Vitreous - persistence of vitreous that normally waste away after birth. Upon completion of the exam, a CHS Eye Examination Certificate will be produced with each condition recorded as Clinically Unaffected or Clinically Affected, except for the graded results for gonioscopy.
