Glossary

Genetics

Genetic selection should rely on genetic tests or health examinations that are reflective of, and associated with, causative genes.

Domestication of dogs has involved selecting for certain characteristics over decades, leading to the breeds recognised today.

Testing is useful to reverse this selection process for some harmful characteristics, and breed more natural looking, healthier dogs.

Genetics of breeding dogs and cats - an explanation/glossary

  • 'Phenotype' describes the physical look of the dog (tests include X-rays, temperament tests, cardiac ultrasound, patella tests)
  • 'Genotype' describes the genetic makeup of the dog (DNA tests from blood or cheek swab)
  • Some genes are passed on in a predictable manner and others less so.
  • The outcome of an autosomal recessive or dominant inheritance is easy to predict.
  • Autosomal just means the gene is carried on a chromosome other than the sex chromosome.
  • Recessive means it has to be present in two copies to cause disease in most cases.
  • Dominant means it only needs one copy to cause disease.
  • Homozygote means two genes the same (affected)
  • Heterozygote means two different (carrier)
  • Coefficient of inbreeding helps predict genetic diversity
  • Estimated Breeding Value exists for some conditions where there has been a lot of testing. It is the likelihood that a dog will pass a condition onto its puppies, based on knowledge of related dogs and their carrier status.

Testing: What do the results mean?

Autosomal recessive explanation

Results of test will be given as 'Clear', 'Carrier' or 'Affected'.

Clear (N/N)

Genotype: N / N [two copies of the normal gene]
This is non-carrier for the mutant gene.
It is very unlikely to develop the disease. They will never pass the mutation to offspring, and therefore the parent can be bred to any other.

Carrier (N/m)

Genotype: N / m [one normal and one mutant gene]
This individual carries one copy of the mutant gene and one copy of the normal gene, so although it is very unlikely it will develop the disease, it can pass it on to half its offspring.
Carriers should only be bred to clear individuals.
Avoid breeding carrier to carrier because 25% of their offspring will be affected.

Affected (m/m)

Genotype: m / m [two copies of the mutant gene]
The individual is likely to develop the disease and will pass the mutant gene to its entire offspring. Breeding to a clear will produce all carriers. Breeding to an affected means all will be affected.

Autosomal dominant explanation

Results of test will be given as'Clear','orAffected'.(no carrier as if the gene is there it is affected).

Clear (N/N)

Genotype:N / N[two copies of the normal gene]

The individual is a non-carrier of the mutant gene.

It is very unlikely to develop the disease and therefore it can be used in breeding. They will never pass the mutation to the offspring, and therefore can be bred to any other individual.

Affected (N / M or M / M)

Affected individuals can have one of two genotypes:

Genotype:N / N[one normal and one mutant gene]

Due to the dominant nature of this condition heterozygous/carrier individuals are likely to develop the disease and show its symptoms.

Genotype:M / M[two copies of the mutant gene ]

Homozygously affected individuals are at very high risk of developing the disease and showing its symptoms more severely than N / M affected ones.

It is not recommended to use affected individuals in breeding

The mutant gene will be transferred to its offspring

The genetics of breeding

Gene Inheritance Patterns

Some genes are passed on in predictable ways, others aren't. Some traits follow clear patterns, others are more complex.

🧬

Autosomal Recessive and Dominant Inheritance

Recessive and dominant inheritance can be predicted. Recessive and dominant traits follow predictable patterns based on the parents' genetics.

🧬

What Does Autosomal Mean?

Autosomal means the gene is on a regular chromosome. These genes aren't linked to gender-determining chromosomes.

🧬

Recessive Inheritance

Recessive traits need two copies to cause disease. Both parents must pass on the gene for the disease to appear.

🧬

Dominant Inheritance

Dominant traits need just one copy to cause disease. A single copy of a dominant gene is enough to show the trait.

🧬

Homozygote (affected)

Two matching genes often result in the disease or trait.

🧬

Heterozygote (carrier)

A mix of genes, often resulting in a carrier without symptoms.

🧬

Coefficient of Inbreeding

Coefficient of inbreeding shows genetic diversity. It measures inbreeding and its potential risks.

What do I do with a Carrier?

Although not ideal, breeding a Carrier is still possible. Breeders may choose to use Carrier cats to preserve valuable breeding lines and maintain genetic diversity. When Carrier cats are mated with Normal cats, no Affected kittens will be born. However, some kittens may be Carriers, which can be identified through genetic testing. These Carrier kittens can then be bred with Normal cats in future matings if needed.
