Glossary

Genetics

Genetic selection should rely on genetic tests or health examinations that are reflective of, and associated with, causative genes.

Domestication of dogs has involved selecting for certain characteristics over decades, leading to the breeds recognised today.

Testing is useful to reverse this selection process for some harmful characteristics, and breed more natural looking, healthier dogs.

Genetics of breeding dogs and cats - an explanation/glossary

  • 'Phenotype' describes the physical look of the dog (tests include X-rays, temperament tests, cardiac ultrasound, patella tests)
  • 'Genotype' describes the genetic makeup of the dog (DNA tests from blood or cheek swab)
  • Some genes are passed on in a predictable manner and others less so.
  • The outcome of an autosomal recessive or dominant inheritance is easy to predict.
  • Autosomal just means the gene is carried on a chromosome other than the sex chromosome.
  • Recessive means it has to be present in two copies to cause disease in most cases.
  • Dominant means it only needs one copy to cause disease.
  • Homozygote means two genes the same (affected)
  • Heterozygote means two different (carrier)
  • Coefficient of inbreeding helps predict genetic diversity
  • Estimated Breeding Value exists for some conditions where there has been a lot of testing. It is the likelihood that a dog will pass a condition onto its puppies, based on knowledge of related dogs and their carrier status.

Testing: What do the results mean?

Autosomal recessive explanation

Results of test will be given as 'Clear', 'Carrier' or 'Affected'.

Clear (N/N)

Genotype: N / N [two copies of the normal gene]
This is non-carrier for the mutant gene.
It is very unlikely to develop the disease. They will never pass the mutation to offspring, and therefore the parent can be bred to any other.

Carrier (N/m)

Genotype: N / m [one normal and one mutant gene]
This individual carries one copy of the mutant gene and one copy of the normal gene, so although it is very unlikely it will develop the disease, it can pass it on to half its offspring.
Carriers should only be bred to clear individuals.
Avoid breeding carrier to carrier because 25% of their offspring will be affected.

Affected (m/m)

Genotype: m / m [two copies of the mutant gene]
The individual is likely to develop the disease and will pass the mutant gene to its entire offspring. Breeding to a clear will produce all carriers. Breeding to an affected means all will be affected.

Autosomal dominant explanation

Results of test will be given as'Clear','orAffected'.(no carrier as if the gene is there it is affected).

Clear (N/N)

Genotype:N / N[two copies of the normal gene]

The individual is a non-carrier of the mutant gene.

It is very unlikely to develop the disease and therefore it can be used in breeding. They will never pass the mutation to the offspring, and therefore can be bred to any other individual.

Affected (N / M or M / M)

Affected individuals can have one of two genotypes:

Genotype:N / N[one normal and one mutant gene]

Due to the dominant nature of this condition heterozygous/carrier individuals are likely to develop the disease and show its symptoms.

Genotype:M / M[two copies of the mutant gene ]

Homozygously affected individuals are at very high risk of developing the disease and showing its symptoms more severely than N / M affected ones.

It is not recommended to use affected individuals in breeding

The mutant gene will be transferred to its offspring

What do I do with a Carrier?

Although not ideal, breeding a Carrier is still possible. Breeders may choose to use Carrier cats to preserve valuable breeding lines and maintain genetic diversity. When Carrier cats are mated with Normal cats, no Affected kittens will be born. However, some kittens may be Carriers, which can be identified through genetic testing. These Carrier kittens can then be bred with Normal cats in future matings if needed.

Information for breeders

Information about the genetics of breeding related to the autosomal recessive condition, spinal muscular atrophy (SMA) in cats.

Key genetic concepts involved:

  • Autosomal Recessive Inheritance: Autosomal recessive traits are genetic conditions that require the presence of two copies of a defective gene (alleles) for the trait to be expressed. In the context of SMA in cats, the condition is caused by a specific gene mutation.

  • Defective Gene: In this case, the defective gene is responsible for causing spinal muscular atrophy. Cats with two copies of the defective gene (homozygous) are affected by the disease.

  • Carrier Cats: Carrier cats have one copy of the defective gene and one copy of the normal gene (heterozygous). They do not show signs of the disease themselves, as the normal gene can partially compensate for the defective one.

  • Breeding Risks: When two carrier cats are bred together, there is a 25% chance that their offspring will inherit two copies of the defective gene, one from each parent, resulting in affected kittens with SMA. There is also a 50% chance that their offspring will inherit one copy of the defective gene from one parent and one copy of the normal gene from the other parent, making them carriers like their parents. The remaining 25% chance is that the offspring will inherit two normal genes and be completely unaffected.

  • Recommendations: Due to the risk of producing affected kittens, it is generally not recommended to intentionally breed two carrier cats together. While breeding two carriers has a higher chance of producing carrier kittens (which don't exhibit symptoms of the disease), the potential for producing affected kittens is a significant concern.

To manage the risk of producing affected kittens, responsible breeders often use genetic testing to determine the carrier status of potential breeding cats. By identifying carriers and non-carriers, breeders can make informed decisions to prevent the unintentional production of kittens with genetic disorders.

Overall, understanding the genetics of breeding and the inheritance patterns of specific genetic traits is crucial for responsible breeding practices and the well-being of the animals involved.

Information for buyers (Layperson explanation)

When cats have kittens, they pass on their traits through genes. Sometimes, there can be a problem gene that causes a sickness such as spinal muscular atrophy (SMA). This gene works like a puzzle piece, and cats need two pieces of the problem gene to get sick.

Now, imagine there are two types of cats: normal cats and carrier cats. Carrier cats have one normal puzzle piece and one problem puzzle piece, but they don't get sick themselves because the normal piece helps to keep them well.

If two carrier cats have kittens, there's a chance their kittens might get two problem puzzle pieces. If this happens, they could get sick with SMA. There's also a chance the kittens could get one problem piece and one normal piece, becoming carriers like their parents but not getting sick.

Because there's a risk of having sick kittens if carrier cats are mated together, it's better to avoid this.

Responsible cat breeders usually do tests to check the puzzle pieces of the cats before they decide to make them parents. This way, they can make sure the kittens are healthy and won't have this SMA problem.

So, to keep kittens healthy, it's important for breeders to know about these genes and how they work when they decide which cats should be parents.

Coefficient of inbreeding (COI)

The Coefficient of Inbreeding (COI) measures the risk that both parents carry the same gene variant, which they could pass to their offspring. The more common ancestors the parents have, the higher the COI. Completely unrelated dogs have a COI of 0. A higher COI increases the chance of passing on a recessive gene for a disorder that might not have a test.

A genetic test gives the most accurate COI. Breeders should compare the COI to the breed average and choose dogs with a COI below the average, ideally less than 5%.

A way to assess the risk of parents passing on complex diseases to puppies, is to use estimated breeding values (EBVs). It tells us how much of a particular condition has been inherited and the likelihood of passing it on to the puppies.

EBVs are only currently available for hip dysplasia and elbow dysplasia in a limited range of breeds. The test involves Xrays, of the joint, being scored for health by a panel of specialists. There is no need for the dog to be KC registered. Currently 28 breeds have EBVs for hip dysplasia and 15 breeds have EBVs for elbow dysplasia. EBVs currently exist for 28 breeds;

Airdale Terrier, Akita, Alaskan Malamute, Bearded Collie, Bernese Mountain Dog, Border Collie, Clumber Spaniel, Dogue de Bordeaux, English Setter, Flat Coated Retriever, German Shepherd Dog, Golden Retriever, Gordon Setter, Hungarian Puli, Hungarian Vizsla, Irish Setter, Italian Spinone, Labrador Retriever, Large Munsterlander, Leonberger, Newfoundland, Old English Sheepdog, Rhodesian Ridgeback, Rottweiler, Samoyed, Siberian Husky, Tibetan Terrier, Weimaraner and Welsh Springer Spaniel.

Six breeds have EBVs for both hip score and elbow grade; Newfoundlands, Labrador Retrievers, Golden Retrievers, German Shepherd Dogs, Rottweilers and Bernese Mountain Dogs.

Where available, EBVs should be used to assist breeding decisions and breeders should adhere to Kennel Club guidance on its Mate Select online facility. Breeders should aim to use dogs with Estimated Breeding Values (EBVs) lower than the average (a negative number) and a confidence rating of at least 60%. EBVs with lower confidence can still be used, but higher confidence means more accuracy.

While lower EBVs are better for reducing the risk of hip/elbow dysplasia, breeders don't need to select dogs with the absolute lowest values. Choosing dogs with lower-than-average EBVs will still reduce risk. Each puppy's EBV is the average of its parents, so a dog with a higher-than-average EBV can still be bred if paired with a dog that has a much lower EBV and good confidence scores for both.

Using EBVs allows for more flexibility compared to older methods that focused on specific hip/elbow scores, which could exclude dogs and reduce genetic diversity. A dog with a higher hip score can still be used if its EBV shows low genetic risk with good confidence, but the offspring should be monitored and scored. Breeding decisions should also consider other factors like temperament, genetic diversity, conformation, and general health.

EBVs and scoring schemes can't guarantee future puppies won't have hip or elbow dysplasia since inheritance is complex. However, using EBVs reduces the risk, though it's not foolproof.

Genetic testing can reliably identify simple recessive disorders (with complete penetrance), helping avoid disease while preserving genetic diversity. Crossbreeding can also help with this.

For dominant gene disorders, affected dogs shouldn't be bred, but carriers can be if paired with a clear mate. Incomplete penetrance means that not all dogs with a mutation will develop the disorder, so testing should focus on conditions that actually affect the breed.

Relying only on genetic testing is not always effective in predicting disease. A negative test can rule out some risks, but knowing if close relatives have the disease is more useful. For example, in Boxers with degenerative myelopathy, many have the gene (47% homozygous, 35% heterozygous), but only a few (0.6%) develop the disease. Outbreeding within the same breed may not always improve genetic diversity, but crossbreeding with a healthy breed can. Tests for mixed-breed dogs should reflect both parent breeds.
