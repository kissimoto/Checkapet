Understanding Breeding and Testing

Mixed breeds are generally healthier than pedigrees. A pedigree certificate is just a family tree. It does not reflect the health of the animal. What really matters is how carefully the breeder chooses which animals to breed. This is where health testing comes in. Good breeders use these tests to avoid passing on genes or physical traits that cause health problems.

What is the current problem CheckAPet is trying to solve?

Decades of inbreeding to achieve a certain look has reduced the gene pool in many popular breeds. This in turn is causing high rates of inherited diseases in purebred dogs, and to a lesser extent cats. Some breeds such as the French Bulldog face a significantly reduced lifespan as a result of their diseases. Shockingly, their life expectancy in the UK is now only four and a half years.

The Dream

The aim is to have a happy, healthy, well adjusted, family pet that can be part of our life for many years to come. No one wants to see a member of the family suffer from a preventable disease.

Points to Bear in Mind

Genetic Health Risk

Some breeds have high genetic disease risk making breeding them ethically questionable. While not all tests are needed for every breed, risk assessment and appropriate testing, helps prevent disease.

Cost of Health Testing

Test prices vary depending on the test. Is it a cheek swab, blood test or an X-ray? Whatever it is, it will be a good investment for the future health and welfare of your new arrival. Don't be fooled, the high price of a puppy or kitten does NOT guarantee good breeding.

Breeding Challenges

Breeding for health is not as straight forward as just having the parents tested. It is far more complex than that. Their physical health at the time of breeding is equally important. Is the mother old, overweight, does she have allergies... It is really important that the parents are health checked by a vet before breeding.

Outcrossing and cross-breeding

Breeding two different breeds (cross-breeding) or unrelated dogs of the same breed (outcrossing) can help broaden the gene pool. However, Kennel and breed clubs often resist this approach in favour of maintaining a defined breed 'standard', which can lead to the repeated breeding of closely related dogs (inbreeding).

Legal responsibility

In the UK dog breeding is regulated to protect animal welfare. Laws like the animal welfare (Licensing of activities involving animals) Regulations 2018 and the Breeding of Dogs Act 1973, require breeders to be licenses and prioritise the health of both parent dogs and puppies. Breeding that could harm an animal's healthb and welfare is not permitted.

How Do I Choose a Family Dog or Cat?

Health testing isn't the golden bullet, but it is a key step to improve the lives of dogs and cats.
A good starting point is understanding what a healthy, moderate looking pet should be.
Pedigree cats are more prone to inherited diseases than 'moggies'. They should not have a full coat and avoid extreme features like flat faces, short legs or folded ears. Sadly, some cats are bred to look more like fantasy creatures than animals.
Dogs too, should avoid extreme traits; no overly long backs, very short legs, flat faces or heavy skin folds. Ears should be a sensible length and ideally upright.
Most importantly pets should breathe easily, sleep soundly, run freely and have a calm, friendly nature to be great family companions.
This site will help guide your decisions and address any questions or concerns you may have.

CheckAPet Expectations and Suggestions

All owners expect a long happy relationship with their pet, and to support this we suggest:

  1. Choose a well bred dog or cat. Breeding from healthy (and health-tested), unrelated animals with normal features is more likely to produce healthy puppies and kittens.
  2. Choose a breeder who has wormed and vaccinated both the parents and offspring. This is a sign of good preventative healthcare. During early development, good breeders gradually expose the young to their environment helping them to be socially resilient.

What Is A Well Bred Dog or Pedigree Cat?

It comes down to a simple checklist:

  1. Health-tested Parents - Before breeding, both parents should undergo physical and genetic testing to ensure they're healthy and not passing on preventable conditions.
  2. Puppy or Kitten Health Check - Both should have a full veterinary health check before you take them homw

How Do I Do This?

  1. All breeding dogs should be tested for diseases common in that breed to the best of knowledge and evidence. Make sure you have copies of these certificates.
  2. Puppy Contract

    This is filled out by you and the breeder. It's a free 7 page document designed to support the responsible breeding and buying of puppies. It covers all the information required when buying a dog.

  3. Be prepared to walk away

    If these stipulations cannot be met or something feels wrong. [This is the most difficult bit - be strong! Buying from bad breeders encourages them to continue. Report any concerns]

  4. Never, never allow delivery of a pet, or collection from a place other than where it was bred and always see the mother.

It is not illegal to breed dogs with extreme features (conformation), but we think it is time for change. Consumers have the power to support responsible breeders and reject those who do not care. Buying from breeders who show low standards of welfare (also known as 'greeders'), encourages them to continue their 'greeding'.

Of course, not all disease can be prevented, but many inherited issues causing illness can be avoided by choosing healthy parents. Some illnesses can be identified by health-test, while others are only visible in the appearance of the animal.

Recommended Documents for all Puppies

Anyone can health-test before breeding. Breeders do not need to be part of a club or society to health test.

Microchip registration document - a legal requirement by 8 weeks of age for puppies, and by 20 weeks for kittens

A signed AWF Puppy Contract
A tool designed to ensure puppies are bred and sold responsibly

Temporary health insurance (4 weeks free)

Vaccination certificate showing vet check of the puppy/kitten and ideally a first vaccination

Copies of the parents health certificate(s)

Details of socialisation, current diet (provide small amount in original bag) and current routine

Frequently Asked Questions

The Kennel Club (KC) registers dog breeds based on a 'Breed Standard' - their idea of an ideal appearance. This focuses on looks rather than health and can encourage extreme features which may compromise welfare. A Kennel Club pedigree certificate simply records the dogs' family tree, it does not guarantee good health.

Cross-breeds are a mix of two different purebreds, whereas a mixed-breed is more like a mutt, sometimes called a Heinz 57 (parentage is not known). The quality of life of cross-breeds may be better than their purebred parents. The term hybrid vigour refers to the possible health benefits of crossbreeding, while "inbred" denotes closely related dog matings. However, some mixes are NOT recommended e.g. Poodle and French bulldog, Sharpei and French bulldog and other crosses that have multiple issues already.

Ideally, health testing should be carried out when crossbreeding. For example Cockerpoos, both the Cocker Spaniel and Poodle parent should be tested for their respective breed-related issues. If any of the subsequent Cockerpoos are bred from, they should be tested too, because undesirable genes may appear in subsequent generations.

  • French bulldogs, pugs and bulldogs are the most commonly discussed brachycephalic dogs.
  • Their most well known condition is the 'brachycephalic obstructive airway syndrome' or BOAS. This describes the obstructed breathing causing a noise called stertor or 'awake snoring'. This is the result of a long soft palate blocking the airway at the back of the throat.
  • They are also commonly affected by all or some of the following; Collapse and exercise intolerance, regurgitation and risk of aspiration pneumonia, sleep apnoea, allergies, exposed and bulging eyes causing corneal damage and ulcers, dry eye, multiple gut diseases, heat Intolerance, skin fold pyoderma, hemi-vertebrae and paralysis/disc disease, orthopaedic disease, multiple myelopathies, chrondrodysplasia (faulty bone and cartilage), dental malocclusions and resultant disease, Chiari-like malformation and syringomyelia, inability to reproduce without caesarean, obesity.

It means to resist the innate human reflex to be attracted to a flat face and big eyes which mimic the layout of a human baby face. The breeding of dogs to look like this, is doing them harm. Key points for breeders:

  • Breeders should select breeding stock with less extreme features and perform pre-breeding genetic health assessments
  • If looking to acquire a short-nosed dog prospective owners must ask breeders what actions they have taken to prevent BOAS in their puppies
  • Parents and puppies should breathe without obvious noise, have open nostrils and a functional muzzle without folds
  • No dog should snore and all should be able to run and breathe freely
  • Selective breeding can return these breeds to a more moderate anatomical conformation and normal breathing

  • Munchkin cats and Scottish Fold cats are breeds which have been developed with genetic mutations that can cause health and welfare issues.
  • Munchkin cats have deliberately shortened legs due to a genetic mutation, which can lead to mobility problems and pain.
  • Scottish Fold cats have a cartilage defect that affects the ears, giving them their characteristic folded appearance, but this same mutation can lead to painful joint conditions and arthritis
