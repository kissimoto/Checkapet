Welcome to CheckAPet

The place to find a healthy, happy new family member and a bright start to life. At CheckAPet we only feature breeders who prioritise health, welfare and temperament.

Health testing is mandatory for breeding dogs and cats, if their offspring are advertised on this site. The offspring will be much more likely to have healthier, and so happier, lives as a result of reducing the risk of inherited disease.

The Consequences of Inbreeding and
Extreme Breeding Practices

Pedigree dogs and cats are not inherently more healthy than non-pedigree. A pedigree certificate is a family tree for that individual, not evidence for health. How healthy or well 'designed' those individuals are depends on how the breeder selects the animals that are to be bred. This is where health testing comes in to the decision-making process, choosing dogs that do not carry genes or physical features that will cause disease in the next generation.

What is the current problem CheckAPet is trying to solve?

Years of inbreeding and breeding for appearance have caused high rates of inherited diseases in purebred dogs, and to a lesser extent in cats. Breeding for extreme features has led to many dogs suffering from painful conditions all their life, such as heart, skin, joint and breathing problems. Some breeds such as the French Bulldog face a significantly reduced lifespan as a result of their disease. Sadly, their life expectancy in the UK is now only four and a half years.

What are the issues?

The dream for someone like you, looking for a family pet, is a happy, well adjusted, healthy individual that can be part of your lives for many years to come. No one wants to see a family member suffer and the heart-break of losing a pet can be a difficult experience.

How do we contribute to fixing them?

Genetic Health Risks

Some breeds have severe genetic diseases that can cause suffering, making breeding them in their current state questionable. Testing the breeding 'parents' for disease and only breeding from those that do not carry genes detrimental to health is a big step in the right direction. Not all tests are essential every time in every breed, but a consideration for the risks of disease and using the tests that can help rule out that disease, are important.

Cost of Health Testing

Health tests before breeding and the costs, vary considerably between breeds depending on the inherited conditions they suffer from. This can affect the cost of a puppy or kitten but will be a good investment for future health and welfare. However, a high price does not guarantee good breeding.

Breeding Challenges

Breeding for health may be complex and can be costly. Trying to eliminate one disease can affect the occurrence of another. There is much more to breeding than genetic testing, the family history of health and disease, and the physical health of the parents are all important.

Outcrossing and cross-breeding

Breeding two or more breeds together (cross-breeding) or breeding two unrelated of the same breed (outcrossing) can help to dilute the gene pool. Kennel or breed clubs often resist this as they are working towards a 'standard' description of a purebred dog. This can result in closely related individuals producing puppies.

Legal responsibility

Breeders are legally responsible for producing healthy dogs under UK law.

How Do I Choose a Family Dog or Cat?

Health testing is not a golden bullet to solve all issues faced by dogs and cats today, but it is a big step in the right direction! Having a clear understanding of what a moderate looking, healthy dog or cat looks like, is a good place to start. Pedigree cats are usually the ones that suffer from inherited disease rather than 'moggies'. They should not have a squashed face, short legs or folded ears and they should have a full coat of fur. It might seem obvious, but there are some poor alien creatures out there! Dogs should not have overly long backs, very short legs, a squashed face or multiple, thick folds of skin. Ears should not be overly long or short, and should ideally stand erect. Dogs and cats should be able to breathe silently, sleep peacefully, run energetically and have a positive, friendly disposition to be an ideal family pet. This site will guide you through the process, helping with considerations and conflicts you may have.

CheckAPet Expectations and Suggestions

All owners expect a long happy relationship with their pet and to support this we suggest

  1. Choose a well bred dog or cat. Breeding from healthy (and health tested), unrelated animals with normal conformation, is more likely to produce healthy puppies and kittens.
  2. Choose a breeder that has practiced preventative healthcare and puppy socialisation. For example, worming and vaccinations are important for both parents and offspring. Gradual exposure to their environment during development helps puppies and kittens to be socially resilient.

What Is A Well Bred Dog (Pedigree Cat)?

It's a relatively simple checklist...

  1. Physical and genetic health tests for the parents, performed before breeding, to check that they are healthy and they don't pass any avoidable problems to the next generation.
  2. A veterinary health check of the puppy before you buy it.

How Do I Do This?

  1. All breeding dogs should be tested for common (and sometimes less common) diseases in that breed to the best of knowledge and evidence. Make sure you have copies of these certificates.
  2. Puppy Contract

    This is filled out by you and the breeder. It is a free 7 page document designed to support the responsible breeding and buying of puppies. It covers all the information required when buying a dog.

  3. Be prepared to walk away

    If these stipulations cannot be met or something feels wrong. [This is the most difficult bit - be strong! Buying from bad breeders encourages them to continue. Report any concerns]

  4. Never allow delivery of a pet or collection from a place other than where it was bred and ALWAYS see the mother

It is not illegal to breed dogs with extreme conformation, but we think it is time for change. Consumers have the power to support responsible breeders and reject those that do not care. Buying from breeders who show low standards of welfare (also known as 'greeders'), encourages them to continue their 'greeding'.

Of course, not all disease can be prevented, but many inherited issues causing illness can be avoided by choosing healthy parents. Some of these can be tested for and some are in the 'look' or 'phenotype' of the dog/cat.

Recommended documents for all puppies

Breeders do not need to be part of a club or society to health test. Testing parents for inherited disease can reduce the chance of these being passed on to future generations. This means those pets will have healthier lives as part of your family. It does not guarantee health, there is more to consider, but it is a start in helping to reduce preventative disease.

Microchip registration document (legal requirement by 8 weeks old for puppies but 5 months old for kittens, so new owner can do this)

A signed AWF Puppy Contract

Temporary health insurance (4 weeks free)

Vaccination certificate showing vet check of the puppy/kitten and ideally a first vaccination

Copies of the parents health certificate(s)

Details of socialisation so far and diet used (provide small amount in original bag). Any current routine that may be helpful for the new owner.

Frequently Asked Questions

The Kennel Club (KC) is a registration of specific dog breeds, designed through breeding over generations, to fit a certain look. The KC describes the 'perfect' example of dog breeds in what is called a 'Breed Standard'. These do not necessarily take into consideration the health of the individual. They may encourage extreme features that are harmful to the welfare of the individuals. The 'Pedigree' is a family tree for the puppy, it does not represent health.

Buying a mixed breed dog may convey some health benefits, although they may not live longer than pedigrees, their quality of life may be better than those with parents exhibiting more exaggerated characteristics. "Hybrid vigor" refers to the possibility of health benefits from crossbreeding, while "inbred" denotes closely related dog matings, reflected in the "coefficient of inbreeding" (COI) on some dog group websites. However, some mixes are NOT recommended e.g. Poodle and French bulldog, Sharpei and French bulldog and other crosses between two breeds that have multiple issues already.

Ideally health testing should be carried out when cross-breeding. For example, Labradoodles or Cockerpoos. Both parents should be tested for their respective breed-related issues. This should apply to the first and subsequent generations since deleterious genes may become apparent in later generations. Health tests for both parents are advisable for cross-breeds too.

There are plenty of responsible breeders out there, even for rare breeds. It's better to choose someone who genuinely cares about the dogs rather than a breeder who disappears when issues arise. Did you know that responsibly bred dogs rarely end up in shelters? Responsible breeders require their puppies to be returned if they can't be cared for. By choosing them, you're supporting people who keep dogs out of shelters and not those who contribute to the problem. If everyone did this, fewer dogs would need rescuing.

  • French bulldogs, pugs and bulldogs are the most commonly discussed brachycephalic dogs.
  • Their most well known condition is the 'brachycephalic obstructive airway syndrome' or BOAS. This describes the obstructed breathing causing a noise called stertor or 'awake snoring'. This is the result of a long soft palate blocking the airway at the back of the throat.
  • They are also commonly affected by all or some of the following; Collapse and exercise intolerance, regurgitation and risk of aspiration pneumonia, sleep apnoea, allergies, exposed and bulging eyes causing corneal damage and ulcers, dry eye, multiple gut diseases, heat Intolerance, skin fold pyoderma, hemi-vertebrae and paralysis/disc disease, orthopaedic disease, multiple myelopathies, chrondrodysplasia (faulty bone and cartilage), dental malocclusions and resultant disease, Chiari-like malformation and syringomyelia, inability to reproduce without caesarean, obesity.

It means to resist the innate human reflex to be attracted to a flat face and big eyes which mimic the layout of a human baby face. The breeding of dogs to look like this, is doing them harm. Key points for breeders:

  • Breeders should select breeding stock with less extreme features and perform pre-breeding genetic health assessments
  • If looking to acquire a short-nosed dog prospective owners must ask breeders what actions they have taken to prevent BOAS in their puppies
  • Parents and puppies should breathe without obvious noise, have open nostrils and a functional muzzle without folds
  • No dog should snore and all should be able to run and breathe freely
  • Selective breeding can return these breeds to a more moderate anatomical conformation and normal breathing

  • Munchkin cats and Scottish Fold cats are breeds which have been developed with genetic mutations that can cause health and welfare issues.
  • Munchkin cats have deliberately shortened legs due to a genetic mutation, which can lead to mobility problems and pain.
  • Scottish Fold cats have a cartilage defect that affects the ears, giving them their characteristic folded appearance, but this same mutation can lead to painful joint conditions and arthritis
