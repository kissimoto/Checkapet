Buying a puppy or kitten

Check-a-pet believe breeders have a moral duty to take sufficient measures to produce healthy puppies/kittens which will grow into healthy, well-balanced adult dogs/cats.

This includes the use of breed-specific tests on both parents before a proposed mating, to reduce the likelihood of the offspring developing the genetic diseases.

DNA testing may be less frequently necessary where multiple generations of a family have been clear of the disease (proved by certification). For single gene mutations carriers may be used for breeding as long as a carrier is mated with a clear partner. This avoids the rapid depletion of the gene pool.

In complex diseases (dogs), where several genes are involved, all dogs considered for breeding should be tested (even if parents of the breeding dogs have scored well). e.g. hip and elbow dysplasia

Talk to your vet

Pre-purchase consultations Your vet can give advice about the type of dog you are interested in. They can discuss the different breeds and help you choose one to compliment your lifestyle. Consider their lifespan and the cost of feeding, treating and looking after them for life.

Talk to the breeder

Take time to find the right fit for your family. Take time to get to know the breeder and how the puppies or kittens have been brought up ....Fail to prepare, prepare to fail.

All new owners should consider the following before buying

For some breeds of dogs where the welfare impact of genetic diseases is so great, it may be considered unethical to continue to breed them in their current extreme state.

Dog breeds are man-made and many have only existed for around150 years.

New breeds continue to be created with subtle changes e.g. in coat colour or texture, these my be linked to disease susceptibility.

Dog breeds have an existing gene pool of pedigree dogs = registered with the Kennel Club and so full ancestry/family tree is known, and purebred dogs = dogs bred using a 'closed gene pool' / close family members, but not necessarily KC registered.

It is possible that in attempting to eliminate one disease one may inadvertently increase the incidence of another.

Carrying out all the relevant breed-related health testing before mating may be costly for a breeder, especially where multiple tests are indicated.

It can be very much more costly for you to have to pay for veterinary treatment for preventable inherited diseases in their dog.

There can be very significant welfare costs for the dog during their life and suffering from inherited disease is not always as obvious as you might think.

It should be noted that the breeder has the option of reflecting the lab costs in the price of puppies from tested parents.

BUT A high price for a puppy does not reflect careful health testing. It may be because a certain type is currently popular or comes from 'champion stock.' Which, unfortunately, is no guarantee of health. Some breeds have several serious inherited health conditions where health testing is not available or can not be expected to make the breed healthier within a reasonable timeframe.

'Outcrossing' to another breed might be the best way to solve some breeds' health issues although kennel clubs and breed clubs are often reluctant to follow this route due to concerns over breed 'purity.'

In the UK, dogs are legally classed as 'goods.' The breeder is the 'producer of these goods' and should do everything to ensure the 'product' is 'fit for purpose,' as a family pet. Consumer Law provides some protection for dog owners under UK law. An owner has every right to expect that the puppy they purchase is genetically sound. Health testing breeding dogs can play a key role in producing healthier puppies.

Recommendations when you buy a puppy

Check-a-pet recommends that puppy buyers thoroughly research the breed they are considering in order to be able to ask a breeder the relevant questions relating to genetic health.

Health testing of dogs used for breeding should be the norm for all breeders, including screening for confirmation-related health and welfare issues. Some dog breeds physical characteristics are detrimental to their health and quality of life. e.g. excessively wrinkled skin, extremely short noses, screw tails, long necks, to name a few.

Health test certificates (copies) should be provided to puppy buyers by the breeder, demonstrating that they are committed to producing healthy puppies and adult dogs. Ask for the breeders local authority license number (they only need to be registered if they breed more than 3 litters a year).

We recommend you do not buy a puppy from abroad. You cannot confirm where it was bred, the conditions they were in or how the mother has been treated.

Download a copy of the puppy contract so you are familiar with the recommendations.

Be prepared to walk away from "Greeders" those breeding for money with no concern for health and welfare.THEY CAN'T SELL IF YOU DON'T BUY.

Frequently Asked Questions

Look for breeders who are registered with their national kennel club, are members of breed-specific clubs, and who perform all recommended health tests for their breed. Good breeders will be happy to answer your questions, show you where the puppies are raised, and introduce you to the parent dogs (at least the mother). They should be concerned about where their puppies are going and may ask you many questions too.

Health tests vary by breed, but may include hip and elbow scoring, eye tests, heart tests, and DNA tests for various genetic conditions. Research the specific health concerns for your chosen breed and ask the breeder for proof that the parents have been tested.

Warning signs include: multiple litters available at once, reluctance to show you where puppies are kept, unwillingness to show the mother with the puppies, selling puppies before 8 weeks of age, no health testing of parent dogs, meeting in public places rather than the breeder's home, and pressure to buy immediately. If something doesn't feel right, it's better to walk away.
