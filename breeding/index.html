Recommended Tests when Breeding

Ensuring the health and wellbeing of future generations starts with proper testing

Health Testing - why bother?

Health testing is part of a comprehensive breeding program. You do not need to be part of a club or breed society to run health tests. Testing the parents for inherited disease reduces the chance of these being passed onto future generations. Those pets will have healthier lives and will be a better family pet. It does not guarantee health, but it is a good start in helping to reduce preventative disease.

For prospective puppy buyers and breeders alike, understanding the potential health issues within each breed is crucial. By prioritising health and collaborating on evidence-based strategies, we can work towards healthier future generations of dogs.

Issues Related to Dog Breeding and Genetic Health

Ethical Concerns in Breeding: Some breeds suffer from severe genetic diseases that cause significant welfare issues. In such cases, it may be considered unethical to continue breeding them in their current form.

Man-Made Nature of Dog Breeds: Many dog breeds have only existed for approximately 150 years. New breeds are still being created, often with minor variations such as coat colour or texture.

Limited Gene Pool & Breeding Practices: Pedigree dogs are registered with the Kennel Club, with a known ancestry. Purebred dogs are bred within a closed gene pool, often involving close family members, but may not always be KC-registered.

Unintended Genetic Consequences: Efforts to eliminate one genetic disease may unintentionally increase the incidence of another.

Cost of Health Testing or Not: Comprehensive breed-specific health testing before mating can be expensive for breeders. Owners may face even higher veterinary costs if their dog develops a preventable inherited disease.

Welfare Costs for the Dogs: The suffering caused by inherited diseases may not always be obvious but can significantly impact the dog's quality of life.

Price vs Health Testing: Breeders can factor health testing costs into puppy prices. However, a high puppy price does not necessarily mean proper health testing has been conductedâ€”it may be due to popularity or champion lineage.

Limitations of Health Testing: Some breeds have multiple serious inherited conditions that cannot be effectively addressed through health testing alone.

Outcrossing as a Solution: Introducing genetic diversity through outcrossing with another breed could improve health. However, kennel and breed clubs often resist this approach due to concerns about maintaining breed purity.

Legal Responsibility of Breeders: In the UK, dogs are legally classified as "goods." Breeders, as "producers" of these goods, have a duty to ensure puppies are fit for purpose as family pets. Consumer law protects owners, giving them the right to expect genetically sound puppies. Health testing plays a crucial role in breeding healthier dogs.

What else should I consider?

While DNA tests can identify genetic markers for specific diseases in the parents, it does not mean they have the disease. They may be carrying the gene, but may not be suffering from the disease themselves. Genetic health testing represents one essential component of responsible breeding, but several additional factors must be considered:

  • Overall Health: Breeding dogs should be free from active conditionsâ€”including skin, gastrointestinal, or joint diseases (even when no specific tests exist).
  • Temperament: Behavioral suitability is equally critical for producing well-adjusted puppies.
  • Veterinary Assessment: A thorough pre-breeding examination should evaluate cardiac health, structural conformation, and other non-genetic factors.

When using genetic health testing, it's important to understand key concepts including:

  • Modes of inheritance
  • Penetrance and prevalence
  • Phenotype expression for specific variants

Breeders must identify testable genetic risks (known variants in their breed) and untestable conditions. Our site helps you to choose the right DNA tests by breed or health condition. Theyâ€™re just one part of responsible breeding.

How do I test?

A blood test (taken by your vet) or a cheek/buccal swab are required (can be done at home). Genetic health testing is not a diagnosis of disease.

Socialisation details

How do young companion animals become resilient within their environment?

    The capacity for puppies and kittens to cope within a complex human environment is highly dependent on pre-birth and very early experiences.

Why is it important?

    So we produce a generation of companion animals that are capable of emotional and behavioural flexibility, as well as enhancing the welfare of their owners.

How to produce resilient and sociable puppies

    Concentration on the emotional flexibility and well-being of puppies, as well as the genetic traits associated with appearance and health, is critical for the emotional welfare of these pets.

    Early and careful socialisation is essential, so that puppies are capable of not only enhancing the lives of their owners, but also living their own best lives.
    To learn to cohabit with dogs or other species, early introduction during the 'socialisation period' is required. This is when their young brain is most responsive to forming social attachments.
    In puppies this is from 3 to 12 weeks old, peaking at 8 weeks old.
    Breeders are therefore responsible for providing a comprehensive introduction of the puppy, to experiences it will encounter within and outside its future home.

Legal considerations

Current UK legislation does not protect puppies or provide penalties for irresponsible breeding. Irresponsible breeding is an important cause of suffering and is frequently associated with preventable breed-related and inherited disease.

Yes

  1. You cannot buy a puppy from a pet shop in the UK. Lucy's Law made it illegal for commercial dealers to sell puppies and kittens unless they had bred the animals themselves. If you want to get a new puppy in England you must now buy directly from the breeder (i.e. see the mother) or adopt from a rescue centre.
  2. All puppies must be microchipped by 8 weeks old (usually at the veterinary health check).
  3. All cats need a microchip but not until 5 months old.
  4. Breeding regulations: Breeders producing more than 3 litters a year (5 in Scotland) must be registered with the local authority. Breeders must meet certain criteria for health, behaviour and welfare at their premises. Link to current legislation in the UK
  5. Mutilations such as ear cropping (which is illegal under the Animal Welfare Act 2006 in England and Wales) and tail docking (only legal for working dogs) are covered by law.
  6. In some countries, breeding 'torture breeds' such as Pugs and French Bulldogs is illegal (e.g. Netherlands).

Ear cropping is a mutilation that impacts a dog's mental and physical health, and welfare. It is illegal in England and Wales under Section 5 of the Animal Welfare Act 2006, and Northern Irish and Scottish equivalents ban the practice unless medically indicated. It is banned in all European States that have ratified the European Convention for the Protection of Pet Animals (Council of Europe 1987).

Declawing is also illegal in the UK. Cats need their claws to express their natural behaviours. This unnecessary procedure to remove the claw and first phalangeal joint leaves them exposed to long term pain.
