Understanding common terms in dog breeding and genetics

Understanding Genetic Terms

This glossary explains common terms used in dog breeding and genetic testing to help you better understand health tests and breeding practices.

Note: Use the search box above to quickly find specific terms or phrases.

The dog has the correct number of copies of the mutation to be affected for the disease/trait being tested for. They will almost certainly develop the disease/trait during their lifetime.

An Acquired Disease is not present at birth but develops later, e.g. due to age.

The mutation is located on one of the autosomes, not on the X or Y sex chromosomes. The disease/trait is not linked to the sex of the animal and affects both males and females equally.

The dog has one copy of the mutation and one normal copy of DNA. These dogs will not develop the tested disease/trait. However, they will pass the mutation on to approximately 50% of their offspring. Carriers only occur with diseases or traits with a recessive mode of inheritance. Furthermore, only females can be carriers of diseases or traits that are X-linked recessive.

The dog has two copies of the normal gene and will not develop the disease/trait being tested for. However, we cannot formally exclude the possibility they might develop a similar condition due to other causes or the effect of other, unidentified mutations.

This is a breed registry or stud book that does not allow outside blood to be introduced to a particular breed of animal. This means that all registered animals and their offspring are descended from the foundation stock, which are the animals that were accepted into the stud book before it closed.

This is the degree to which a puppy is 'inbred', how closely related are its parents. The higher the number the more inbred the puppy. A mother to son breeding would have a COI of 25. The lower the better, below 12.5 has been recommended.

A Congenital Disease is one the puppy is born with.

This is an indication of how much scoring data has been used to calculate the EBV. The more scoring information available, from the dog itself and/or its relatives, the more confident we are that the EBV, as a percentage, is close to the actual genetic risk. The confidence of the EBV can increase if more relatives are, or the dog itself is scored.

For example:

  • A dog has its own hip score, but no relatives are scored, will have a confidence of about 60%
  • A dog with no hip score, but both parents were scored, will have a confidence of about 40%
  • A dog with no hip score and only one parent scored, will have a confidence of about 30%

The disease/trait will affect the dog whether it carries ONE or TWO copies of the mutation. With one copy of the mutation (termed a heterozygote), they will pass the mutation on to approximately 50% of their offspring, causing them to be affected.

EBV is a statistical technique that calculates how likely an individual is to have a disease based on the family history or pedigree. The breed average is set at 0 and the score for an individual is higher if the risk is above average to pass on the genes or negative if less likely than average to pass them on. The further away from 0 the higher or lower its genetic risk.

As more data becomes available, the breed average may change and a dog's EBV will change accordingly. At birth the puppy will be an average of the two parents (e.g., if dam is -8 and sire is +8 then the puppy will be 0, this is average genetic status for the breed, but no reflection of the health of the e.g. hips, the hips score is needed in this instance to reflect that.

'Genotype' describes the genetic makeup of the dog (DNA tests from blood or cheek swab).

Genotype refers to the genetics of an individual, which may not be visible, e.g., polycystic kidneys.

A Hereditary Disease is one which is passed genetically from the parents, but which may not be expressed as a disease in the puppy.

'Phenotype' describes the physical look of the dog (tests include X-rays, temperament tests, cardiac ultrasound, patella tests).

Phenotypic refers to physical characteristics visible on body, e.g., blue eyes.

The disease/trait will affect the dog only when they carry TWO copies of the mutant DNA. If the dog carries one copy of an autosomal mutation (CARRIER) it will not be affected. However, they will pass the mutation on to approximately 50% of their offspring.

Penetrance refers to the proportion of individuals with a specific genetic mutation who exhibit the clinical symptoms of the associated disease or trait. It describes how frequently a genotype (genetic makeup) is expressed in the phenotype (physical characteristics).

Complete penetrance: When all individuals carrying the genetic mutation show the associated symptoms or traits (100% penetrance).

Incomplete penetrance: When some individuals carrying the genetic mutation do not show the associated symptoms or traits. For example, a disease with 70% penetrance means that 70% of individuals with the mutation will develop the disease, while 30% will not, despite having the same genetic mutation.

Variable penetrance: When the expression of the genetic trait varies in severity or age of onset among individuals with the same genetic mutation.

The mutation for the disease/trait is located on the X chromosome and thus affects males and females differently. Females will have 2 copies of the X-linked gene whereas males only have one copy. As a result, males with a single copy of the mutated gene will develop the disease or trait whether it is DOMINANT or RECESSIVE.
