Understanding common terms in dog or cat breeding and genetics

Understanding Genetic Terms

This glossary explains common terms used in breeding and genetic testing to help you better understand health tests and breeding practices.

Note: Use the search box above to quickly find specific terms or phrases.

An Acquired Disease is not present at birth but develops later, e.g. due to age.

Autosomes are all the other chromosomes that are not the sex chromosomes (X and Y). Sex chromosomes determine the gender, whereas autosomes determine various traits, biological processes and susceptibility to disease.

Autosomal refers to genes located on the autosomes. In dogs there are 38 pairs of autosomes (2 x 38 = 76) plus the two sex chromosomes (X and Y), totaling 78, and in cats there are 18 pairs of autosomes, totaling 38 chromosomes including X and Y.

When a mutation is autosomal, it means the mutation is located on one of these non-sex chromosomes. As a result, the disease or trait is not linked to the animal's sex and affects both males and females equally.

This is in contrast to sex-linked traits, which are carried on the X or Y chromosome and may affect males and females differently (such as X-linked conditions that often affect males more severely than females).

The animal has one copy of the mutation and one normal copy of DNA. These animals will not develop the tested disease/trait. However, they will pass the mutation on to approximately 50% of their offspring. Carriers only occur with diseases or traits with a recessive mode of inheritance. Furthermore, only females can be carriers of diseases or traits that are X-linked recessive.

The animal has two copies of the normal gene and will not develop the disease/trait being tested for. However, we cannot formally exclude the possibility they might develop a similar condition due to other causes or the effect of other, unidentified mutations.

A closed stud book refers to a breed registry system that does not allow the adding of new genetic contribution to a particular breed of animal. This means that all registered animals and their offspring are descended from the foundation stock, which are the animals that were accepted into the stud book before it closed.

This is the degree to which a puppy is 'inbred', how closely related are its parents. The higher the number the more inbred the puppy. A mother to son breeding would have a COI of 25. The lower the better, below 12.5 has been recommended.

A Congenital Disease is one the puppy is born with.

This is an indication of how much scoring data has been used to calculate the EBV. The more scoring information available, from the animal itself and/or its relatives, the more confident we are that the EBV, as a percentage, is close to the actual genetic risk. The confidence of the EBV can increase if more relatives are, or the dog itself is scored.

For example:

  • A dog has its own hip score, but no relatives are scored, will have a confidence of about 60%
  • A dog with no hip score, but both parents were scored, will have a confidence of about 40%
  • A dog with no hip score and only one parent scored, will have a confidence of about 30%

The disease/trait will affect the dog whether it carries ONE or TWO copies of the mutation. With one copy of the mutation (termed a heterozygote), they will pass the mutation on to approximately 50% of their offspring, causing them to be affected.

Estimated Breeding Values (EBVs) predict whether an animal is more or less likely to have, and pass on, genes related to a particular health problem. EBVs link information about an animal's family history with data on whether they, or their relatives, have a specific health issue and to what degree.

How EBVs work:

  • The breed average is set at 0
  • A positive value (above 0) indicates higher risk than average
  • A negative value (below 0) indicates lower risk than average
  • The further away from 0, the higher or lower the genetic risk

For example, if a mother (dam) has an EBV of -8 (low risk) and a father (sire) has an EBV of +8 (high risk), their puppy will have an EBV of 0 (average risk). As more data becomes available, the breed average may change and an animal's EBV will be updated accordingly.

EBVs help breeders make more informed decisions by considering not just an individual animal's test results, but also the health history of their entire family.

'Genotype' refers to the genetic makeup of an animal - the actual genes it possesses. Genotype testing is done through DNA analysis from blood samples or cheek swabs.

An animal's genotype may include traits that aren't visibly expressed. For example, a labrador with a normal black coat (phenotype) might carry the recessive "yellow coat" gene (genotype) and could pass this on to puppies if bred with another carrier.

Testing Methods: Genotypic testing takes place in DNA labs through blood tests or buccal (cheek) swabs.

Why It Matters: DNA tests predict potential health risks by identifying the presence of specific genes, even when the animal shows no visible signs of disease.

A disorder caused by mutations (changes) in certain genes or chromosomes that are passed down from parent to offspring. While an animal inherits the genetic mutation, it may not always show symptoms of the disease.

Examples of purely hereditary diseases include:

  • Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD): A condition involving fluid-filled cysts in the kidneys leading to kidney failure, more common in cats than dogs.
  • Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM): A heart condition where the heart muscle thickens, prevalent in cats and less common in dogs.
  • von Willebrand Disease: A bleeding disorder caused by a deficiency in a specific clotting factor, more common in dogs than cats.
  • Muscular Dystrophy: A group of inherited diseases characterized by progressive muscle weakness and degeneration.
  • Mucopolysaccharidosis (MPS): A group of inherited metabolic disorders affecting various organs and tissues.

Unlike acquired diseases, hereditary conditions cannot be contracted through exposure to pathogens or environmental factors - they are exclusively passed through genes.

'Phenotype' refers to the observable physical characteristics or traits of an animal - what you can actually see or measure. This includes appearance, behavior, and other measurable traits.

Examples of phenotypic traits include coat color (like blue eyes), body size, and physical health conditions that can be observed or detected through examinations.

Testing Methods: Phenotypic testing takes place during veterinary examinations, including X-rays, cardiac ultrasounds, eye examinations, and other physical assessments.

Why It Matters: Physical examinations confirm an animal's current health status and visible traits, but don't always reveal genetic carriers of diseases.

Genotype vs. Phenotype Example: A Labrador might have the genotype for both black and yellow coat colors, but its phenotype will only show one color depending on which genes are dominant. Similarly, an animal can carry disease genes (genotype) without showing any symptoms (phenotype).

The disease/trait will affect the animal only when they carry TWO copies of the mutant DNA. If the animal carries one copy of an autosomal mutation (CARRIER) it will not be affected. However, they will pass the mutation on to approximately 50% of their offspring.

Penetrance refers to the proportion of individuals with a specific genetic mutation who exhibit the clinical symptoms of the associated disease or trait. It describes how frequently a genotype (genetic makeup) is expressed in the phenotype (physical characteristics).

Complete penetrance: When all individuals carrying the genetic mutation show the associated symptoms or traits (100% penetrance).

Incomplete penetrance: When some individuals carrying the genetic mutation do not show the associated symptoms or traits. For example, a disease with 70% penetrance means that 70% of individuals with the mutation will develop the disease, while 30% will not, despite having the same genetic mutation.

Variable penetrance: When the expression of the genetic trait varies in severity or age of onset among individuals with the same genetic mutation.

The mutation for the disease/trait is located on the X chromosome and thus affects males and females differently. Females will have 2 copies of the X-linked gene whereas males only have one copy. As a result, males with a single copy of the mutated gene will develop the disease or trait whether it is DOMINANT or RECESSIVE.
