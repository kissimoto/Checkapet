Kitten Contract

Contract between breeders and buyers

Health testing is mandatory for breeding cats if their offspring are advertised on this site. The offspring will be much more likely to have healthier, and so happier, lives as a result of reducing the risk of inherited disease.

Important Note for Moggie Owners:

Most genetic testing requirements in this contract apply to pedigreed cats. For moggies (non-pedigreed cats), the key health requirements are: up-to-date vaccinations, worming, microchipping, and basic health checks. The inherited conditions section may not be applicable unless specific breed heritage is known.

Q16: Is the mother up to date with UK vaccinations?
It is very important that the mother's routine vaccinations are up to date. She needs to be vaccinated to make sure her kittens are born with a good immunity to certain deadly diseases. This will keep them healthy until they can be vaccinated themselves. Ask your breeder what vaccines she should have had.
Q17: When was the mother last treated for worms and which product was used?
Regular worming of the mother, including during pregnancy, is important to make sure the kittens are not born infested with worms. Ask your breeder about how effective the products listed are, and whether they are up to date. Avoid buying from breeders who do not vaccinate or worm their animals adequately.
Q18: Has either parent had any surgical procedures to correct features that could be inherited by the kitten?
Many breeds have been bred to emphasise certain features which over time have become more and more exaggerated. Although 'normal' for a breed, flat faces, heavy wrinkles and very small ears are just a few examples of features that may cause problems. For example, cats with short flat faces often have narrow nostrils and tiny windpipes. They can suffer severe breathing difficulties and may even have difficulty enjoying a walk or playing. Folded or wrinkled skin may be itchy and painful, and infolding eyelids can scratch the eye ball. Some of these problems will require lifelong medication or sometimes surgery. You should also be told if either parent has had surgery to fix a problem such as eyelids which rubbed on the eye as this may no longer be obvious to see, but could still be inherited by the kitten.
Q19: Before the sale will the new owner have the chance to interact with the kitten's parents and other kittens in the litter if there are any?
It is very important that you see the kitten's mother and the other kittens in the litter. You should also try and see the father if possible, although this may be difficult in some circumstances. Make sure you interact with the parents, handle them if safe to do so, and assess their reaction to you. Check they appear healthy, check the environment that the mother and kittens are in and check the temperament of the mother, father and other littermates. The parents' characteristics can influence how the kittens develop and behave. In particular, look for signs of fear, such as withdrawal, cowering, urination, and tail between the legs. Also watch for signs of aggression such as growling, lunging and hissing.
Unscrupulous breeders can try and show you another cat instead of the kitten's mother. To avoid this make sure you see the mother and kittens together, and check for signs that the cat you see has given birth recently, such as having enlarged nipples. Ask why other littermates or father cannot be seen if breeders answer 'no' in these sections.
Q20: How many litters has the mother had, including this one?
Pregnancy, birth and rearing kittens all take a lot of energy and work for any mother. She may have problems such as difficulty giving birth and poor body condition if her nutritional needs aren't met. Mothers should be allowed at least one season between litters. Avoid kittens from mothers that have had numerous litters, as this may be a sign that the breeder has expected too much from the mother and may also mean that the care and condition of the kitten might not be ideal either.
Q21: How old was the mother when she had her first litter and was it on her first season?
Responsible breeders should not breed from their cats during their first season and should not breed from cats under about one year old. This makes sure the mother is fully grown, mature and is as capable as possible of coping with pregnancy and birth. Breeders who have ignored these laws or guidelines may not be very knowledgeable and caring about the mother, and therefore the kitten.

Pedigree and Relationship Information

Q27: If the kitten's pedigree is known and a pedigree certificate will be attached
The pedigree certificate shows the kitten's ancestry and may go back up to five generations, starting with the kitten's parents in the left column through to the great-great-great grandparents on the right column. The top half shows the kitten's ancestry through its father and the bottom half is the mother's pedigree. You can see an example of a certificate at CheckAPet.vet.
Q28: What is the relationship between the kitten's parents?
If the kitten's parents are related to each other then the kitten is inbred. The degree of inbreeding depends on how closely related the parents are. If an ancestor's name is repeated in both the father's and the mother's halves of the pedigree certificate then there is inbreeding.
Inbreeding is not desirable as it increases the risk of inherited disease. Avoid a kitten from matings between first or second degree relatives e.g.
■ First degree – mother/son, father/daughter, brother/sister
■ Second degree – uncle/niece, aunt/nephew, grandparent/grandchild
Q29: How inbred is the kitten?
It's important to avoid buying kittens that are very inbred because they have a higher chance of having inherited diseases and being unwell later in life. A simple way to describe this is the Coefficient of inbreeding (COI). This is a number where the higher the number, the more inbred the kitten.
For example, a very inbred kitten would come from a mother to son mating or a brother to sister, both having a COI of 25.
Kittens with any degree of inbreeding should be avoided. Choose unrelated parents for the healthiest kittens.
Kittens whose background is not known cannot have a score calculated and in these cases the box will be left blank.
The COI for registered cats can be found using breed registry Mate Select services.

You can also use Mate Select to find out the average COI for particular breeds. For more information: CheckAPet.vet

Inherited Disorders and Screening Tests

Q30: Inherited disorders
Inherited disorders
Many breeds of cat are prone to a number of inherited disorders. Pedigreed cat registration and showing rules mean that breeds not only look different but they also have higher likelihoods of developing particular disorders. Crossbred cats can also inherit disorders from their parents' breeds.
Some inherited conditions are very rare or cause only minor suffering, whilst others are extremely painful or life threatening. Examples of inherited disorders are cancer, blindness, diabetes, heart disease, skin complaints, epilepsy, and deafness.
It is important to be aware of these conditions as they could have been passed on to the kitten.
Talk to your vet before you buy and check for information on inherited disorders at: CheckAPet.vet
DNA and health screening tests
Several health screening tests are available to measure the risk that a cat will be affected by the disease, be a carrier (and able to pass it on to their offspring) or be unaffected.

Future Care and Welfare

Q32: What food is the kitten currently eating?
Q33: What type of accommodation does the kitten need?
Different breeds and individual cats have different needs. Some cats are happy living indoors, while others need access to outside spaces. Some breeds require more mental stimulation and play than others. Ask your breeder about the specific needs of your kitten's breed and individual temperament.
Q34: What ongoing veterinary care will the kitten need?
All cats need regular veterinary care including vaccinations, parasite prevention, dental care, and health check-ups. Some breeds may need additional monitoring for specific health conditions.
Q35: What exercise and mental stimulation does the kitten need?
Cats need appropriate exercise and mental stimulation to stay healthy and happy. This includes play time, climbing opportunities, scratching posts, and interactive toys. Some breeds are more active than others and may need more stimulation.
Q36: What grooming will the kitten need?
Different breeds have different grooming requirements. Long-haired cats need daily brushing, while short-haired cats may need less frequent grooming. Some breeds are prone to eye discharge or ear problems that need regular cleaning.
Q37: Will the kitten need to be neutered and when?
Unless you plan to breed from your cat, neutering is recommended to prevent unwanted pregnancies and reduce the risk of certain health problems. Most cats are neutered between 4-6 months of age, but timing may vary based on breed and individual circumstances.

Contract for the Sale and Purchase of a Kitten

Definitions

  • "Kitten" means the cat described in the Kitten Information Pack.
  • "Kitten Information Pack" means any document or documents supplied by the Seller to the Buyer at any time that sets out the Cat Health Information and Future Health and Welfare Needs.
  • "Cat Health Information" means the information about the kitten, its parents and grandparents, and these animals' health and temperament.
  • "Good Health" means a condition of health free of parasites and congenital defects and of reasonably sound physical condition and temperament.
  • "Future Health and Welfare Needs" means the measures that should be taken to ensure that the Kitten is and remains in Good Health and is properly housed, fed, watered, trained, socialised and exercised and that it receives appropriate veterinary attention.

1. The Seller warrants:

1.1 that if the Buyer has previously selected the Kitten from a litter or in any other circumstances, that the Kitten sold under this contract is the kitten that the Buyer has previously selected.
1.2 that the genetic health checks and health screen set out in the Kitten Information Pack have been carried out on the Kitten's parents and the results of these have been fully explained to the Buyer.
1.3 that the Kitten is not the result of a mating of two individuals related within two generations.
1.4 that the Kitten is in Good Health, other than as the Seller specifically informs the Buyer before the date of sale.
1.5 that he/she has supplied the Kitten Information Pack prior to the Buyer viewing the Kitten or a reasonable period in advance of the Buyer's decision to buy the Kitten and has communicated to the buyer:
a) the ramifications of buying the Kitten given the Cat Health Information, the genetic health checks and health screening carried out under clause 1.2 and the particular considerations that are likely to affect the Kitten given its breed;
b) the implications of any breeding restrictions;
c) the measures that should be taken, whether by neutering, contraception or otherwise, to guard against unwanted pregnancy; and
d) the Future Health and Welfare Needs of the Kitten.
1.6 that the information contained in the Kitten Information Pack supplied to the Buyer under clause 1.5 above is accurate and materially complete (to the best of the Seller's knowledge and belief where the information is supplied by a third party) and all diagnostic tests for the kitten have been undertaken as recorded in the Cat Health Information and that reasonable care and skill is and/or will be used when explaining to the Buyer the Future Health and Welfare Needs of the Kitten and any advice or recommendations provided under clause 2.5 below.
1.7 that he/she, having made reasonable enquiries of the Buyer, in good faith believes that the Buyer is able to meet the Kitten's Future Health and Welfare Needs.
1.8 that the Kitten is more than 8 weeks old at the date of sale.
1.9 that the Kitten has received adequate care and that the Seller has provided it with the opportunity to socialise with cats, humans and other animals it is likely to come into contact with and it has been habituated to the sounds and experiences of typical family life.
1.10 that, where appropriate, the Seller has provided the Buyer with all relevant registration papers and pedigree certificate and that any pedigree indicated for the Kitten is accurately stated.

2. The Buyer warrants:

2.1 that he/she understands the information provided to him/her under clauses 1.2-1.5 above.
2.2 that he/she in good faith believes that he/she will be able, and intends, to meet the Kitten's Future Health and Welfare Needs.
2.3 that neither he/she nor any member of his/her household has been cautioned for or convicted of any breach of animal welfare law such as neglect, cruelty or abandonment. Under the Animal Welfare Act 2006 (as amended in 2021), the maximum penalty for cruelty offences is five years imprisonment and/or an unlimited fine.
2.4 that he/she shall not act in any way contrary to any breeding restrictions.
2.5 that in the event that he/she is no longer able or willing to provide a home for the Kitten or otherwise to meet the Kitten's Future Health and Welfare Needs he/she will contact the Seller and have regard to any advice and recommendations that the Seller provides.

3. Both Buyer and Seller agree and understand:

3.1 that the Kitten is a living creature with interests independent of both Buyer and Seller.
3.2 that in the event of the Kitten suffering any pain or discomfort as a result of any breach of the obligations set out in this Agreement the party not in breach shall be entitled to recover from the party in breach damages for such distress and inconvenience as the Party not in breach can establish he/she has suffered.
3.3 nothing in this contract affects the Buyer's statutory rights including any warranty of satisfactory quality of the Kitten implied by sale of goods legislation or other law.

Animal Welfare Act Requirements

All cat owners must ensure their cats have the five welfare needs:

  • A suitable environment
  • A suitable diet
  • The ability to exhibit normal behaviour patterns
  • The opportunity to be alone
  • Protection from pain, suffering, injury and disease

Under the Animal Welfare Act 2006 (as amended in 2021), the maximum penalty for cruelty offences is five years imprisonment and/or an unlimited fine. The minimum age to purchase a cat is 16 years.

To report cruelty: England - RSPCA, Scotland - SSPCA (03000 999 999), Northern Ireland - USPCA (028 3025 1000)
