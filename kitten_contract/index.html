Inbreeding

If the kitten's parents are related to each other then the kitten is inbred. Inbreeding is not desirable as it increases the risk of inherited disease. Avoid a kitten from matings between first or second degree relatives:

  • First degree – mother/son, father/daughter, brother/sister
  • Second degree – uncle/niece, aunt/nephew, grandparent/grandchild
Coefficient of Inbreeding (COI)

This is a number where the higher the number, the more inbred the kitten. For example, a very inbred kitten would come from a mother-to-son or brother-to-sister mating with a COI of 25.

Kittens with any degree of inbreeding should be avoided. Choose unrelated parents for the healthiest kittens.

6. Inherited Disorders and Screening Tests

Inherited Disorders

Many breeds of cat are prone to inherited disorders. Some conditions are rare or cause minor suffering, while others are extremely painful or life-threatening. Examples include cancer, blindness, diabetes, heart disease, skin complaints, epilepsy, and deafness.

DNA and Health Screening Tests

Several health screening tests are available to measure the risk that a cat will be affected by disease, be a carrier, or be unaffected.

7. Future Care and Welfare

Accommodation Needs

Different breeds and individual cats have different needs. Some cats are happy living indoors, while others need access to outside spaces. Some breeds require more mental stimulation and play than others.

Veterinary Care

All cats need regular veterinary care including vaccinations, parasite prevention, dental care, and health check-ups. Some breeds may need additional monitoring for specific health conditions.

Exercise and Mental Stimulation

Cats need appropriate exercise and mental stimulation including play time, climbing opportunities, scratching posts, and interactive toys. Some breeds are more active than others.

Grooming

Different breeds have different grooming requirements. Long-haired cats need daily brushing, while short-haired cats may need less frequent grooming. Some breeds are prone to eye discharge or ear problems.

Neutering

Unless you plan to breed from your cat, neutering is recommended to prevent unwanted pregnancies and reduce health risks. Most cats are neutered between 4-6 months of age.

Breeder Support

A good breeder should offer ongoing support and be available to answer questions about your kitten's care, health, and behavior. They should also be willing to take the kitten back if you can no longer care for it.

3. About the Mother

Vaccinations

It is very important that the mother's routine vaccinations are up to date. She needs to be vaccinated to make sure her kittens are born with a good immunity to certain deadly diseases. This will keep them healthy until they can be vaccinated themselves.

Worming

Regular worming of the mother, including during pregnancy, is important to make sure the kittens are not born infested with worms. Ask your breeder about how effective the products listed are.

Surgery for Inherited Features

Many breeds have been bred to emphasise certain features which over time have become exaggerated. Flat faces, heavy wrinkles and very small ears can cause problems. You should be told if either parent has had surgery to fix problems that could be inherited.

Meeting the Parents

It is very important that you see the kitten's mother and other kittens in the litter. Check they appear healthy and assess their temperament. Unscrupulous breeders may try to show you another cat instead of the kitten's mother.

Age at First Litter

Responsible breeders should not breed from their cats during their first season and should not breed from cats under about one year old.
